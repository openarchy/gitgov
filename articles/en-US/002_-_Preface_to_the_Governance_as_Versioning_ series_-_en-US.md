## **Preface to the Governance as Versioning series**

# **Author's note: a mind shaped like a crooked tree**

*This preface is a personal note about how the project was born. It is not an article in the series: it is the conversation before things begin. If you prefer to go straight to the content, the Introduction that follows explains where the series starts and what you will find in each part.*

---

My way of thinking has always been an untrimmed tree. Branches going everywhere. Leaves where they shouldn't be. And the constant feeling that there was a missing node — a connection I wasn't seeing.

It bothered me. Everything I learned seemed to float without a place to land. Psychology on one branch, technology on another, politics hanging from a root that never touched the ground. A mental map that never closed.

The advice was always the same: mindfulness, meditation, breathing. I tried. For years. The standard explanation? Emotional. "You think too much, Gustavo."

Today I see it differently. It wasn't excess thought. It was fuel with no engine to burn it. My mind kept accelerating because it wanted to fit everything it learned into a single place. And no such place existed.

Until life did its favorite trick.

On a Saturday night, I was doing what I do best: complaining with conviction about how the entire world was dysfunctional. My classic monologue. Rafaela (biologist, teacher, center-left progressive, calm the way someone is when they already know they're going to win the argument) listened. Sighed. And said:

"Yeah, Gustavo… Nobody's found a solution for that yet. If you have one, it'll be a real contribution!"

Pause. And then came the part that mattered: she didn't wait for me to finish complaining. She asked what I would do differently. Not in theory. Concretely. With whom. Starting where.

I explained. She listened. And agreed with me.

Yes. It happens. Planetary alignment. Once every 76 years. Halley feelings.

But what stayed with me wasn't the agreement. It was the question. What would you do differently? That's not a rhetorical question. It's the question that forces you to stop complaining and start building. It was what unlocked the project.

In that instant the crooked tree aligned. I saw the trunk. I saw the roots. I saw the whole design.

And the design was simple: the problems we see in the world — polarization, corruption, decisions no one knows who made, rules no one knows who wrote — are not the problem. They are symptoms. The real problem is architectural. The way decisions are made, recorded, corrected, and shared is broken at the base.

The solution already exists. It just wasn't where most people look.

## **1. The most unknown technology of the digital age**

In April 2005, a frustrated Finnish programmer built a tool in less than two weeks. Today, twenty years later, that tool coordinates practically everything you use on your phone, your computer, the internet, and behind the scenes of your bank, your favorite store, your messaging app. Its name is Git.

If you ask a hundred people on the street what Git is, ninety-eight will say: "Never heard of it." Those same people use apps, websites, and services that only exist because millions of developers use Git every day.

Git is invisible. And it is, perhaps, the most influential technology that almost no one knows about.

What it does is simple to explain: Git is a system where many people work together on the same project without needing a central boss. Every change is recorded — who made it, when, and why. Any mistake can be undone. Anyone who disagrees can go their own way without destroying the original. And merit is proven by visible contribution, not by title.

Git is, at its core, a decision history with an undo button.

The Linux operating system (which runs on over 90% of the world's servers) works this way. Wikipedia operates on similar logic. Thousands of people collaborating with no rigid hierarchy, no mandatory voting, no forced consensus. And it works. It has worked for two decades, with millions of lines of code, at a scale no company has managed to match.

In the 90s, when someone mentioned email, the response was: "I'd rather send a letter. That'll never catch on." The same happened with instant messages, video calls, social networks. Every technology that changed the world went through a phase where it seemed like a niche thing. Git is exactly at that point. Except the barrier is not skepticism: it is language. The names are intimidating. The tutorials skip the basics. The jargon excludes.

People are not lacking in intelligence. They are lacking a translation.

## **2. What this series proposes**

This series of 21 articles provides that translation.

It takes 335 years of political, philosophical, and systemic thought — from John Locke in 1689 to Ethan Mollick in 2024 — and shows that the most powerful ideas about how to organize people, correct mistakes, and distribute power converge on a model that developers already use every day without thinking twice.

The thesis fits in one sentence: governing is versioning. Erring is recording. Correcting is merging. Authority is peer review.

This is not a pretty metaphor. It is a system with 21 concrete steps that any group can apply — a neighborhood association, a cooperative, a school, a homeowner association — without a computer, without a developer, without permission.

Before the 21 numbered articles, there is an Introduction that tells the origin of the tool, and three complementary pieces outside the numbering: the glossary with 22 fundamental concepts, the 21-step protocol, and the text "You never start from zero," which uses an everyday situation to introduce the system to anyone who has never heard of Git. The series proper opens with Locke. But the philosophical background of the tool — Richard Stallman and the four freedoms of free software, Eric Raymond and the difference between cathedral and bazaar, Linus Torvalds and the building of Linux — appears in the Introduction that follows this preface.

The 21 articles cover three movements. First, the foundations: Locke and consent, Popper and the right to be wrong, Hayek and the knowledge no central committee can assemble, Shannon and the difference between information and noise, Dawkins and the ideas that survive not because they are true, but because they are good at replicating themselves. Then, the limits: Weber's iron cage, Kahneman's biases, Wurman's information anxiety, Ostrom's eight principles for managing resources that belong to everyone. Finally, coordination at scale: how crowds can be smarter than experts, how peer production works without a boss, how the code that governs a system is the group's real constitution, how networks define who has power and who is invisible.

It ends with warnings. Because every open system attracts those who want to close it. Yarvin and the authoritarian risk. Land and technocracy. Thiel and the temptation of capture. And with a door: Mollick and the question of what happens when artificial intelligence enters the game.

## **3. The spark**

I have always been driven by that quiet hunger to understand the world from the inside. I never followed an official path. One book pulled another, one concept pulled another. I followed the scent of curiosity without asking permission for disciplines to talk to each other.

I spent years building data systems. I learned something that seems technical but is universal: the way you organize information determines what people can do with it. A well-designed system lets ordinary people do extraordinary things. A poorly designed system makes even competent people fail.

It is not a question of who operates it. It is a question of how the system was built.

I began to notice the same was true for collective decisions. Meetings where no one records anything. Votes with no history. Decisions that vanish in three months because no one wrote down the terms. Competent people producing bad results, not from incompetence, but because the architecture of the process was broken.

And then came the question that wouldn't leave me: if it works for code, why wouldn't it work for human decisions?

Kathryn Schulz wrote that each person projects a different constellation onto the same sky, based on their own history. My sky was chaotic. But coherent. The branches of the crooked tree were all there for a reason. Psychology explained why people make twisted decisions. Biology explained how collaboration emerges without a coordinator. Technology offered the missing tool. Philosophy provided the foundation.

All it took was someone nudging me on a Saturday night to see the complete picture.

"Stay hungry. Stay foolish." Jobs popularized that phrase in his Stanford speech, but it was born in the Whole Earth Catalog, that semi-anarchist publication from the 70s that encouraged free intellectual exploration. It captures my style: hungry, curious, reckless in just the right measure.

I wasn't too anxious. I was too curious to fit in the box.

## **4. How to read this series**

Each article works on its own. You can read them in order or jump to the thinker who interests you most. But there is a thread connecting all of them: the idea that no one should govern without being revisable, no decision should exist without a record, and no system should be too large to be corrected.

If at any point you think "this makes sense for my reality," the text has done its job.

If you disagree with everything and want to make your own version: even better. That is called a fork. And that is exactly how this system was designed to work.

This system exists to be surpassed. If it becomes absolute truth, it has failed.

Now, let us go back 335 years. England, 1689. A doctor who never practiced medicine is about to turn the concept of power upside down. And without knowing it, he will write the first revert in history.

But before diving into the thinkers, the introduction that follows tells where this series began: three frustrations, three responses, and a tool that changed the world without almost anyone noticing.
