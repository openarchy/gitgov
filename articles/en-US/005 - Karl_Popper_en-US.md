**What you'll find here.**

Have you ever watched a decision be made with such certainty that no one dared ask "what if it goes wrong"? A vote where the result was announced before the debate? A meeting where asking questions was seen as disloyalty?

That's the pattern Popper spent his entire life trying to understand. And destroy.

## **0. The man who died for doubting in public.**

Athens, 399 BC.

A seventy-year-old man stands before five hundred judges. He is not accused of a crime with a victim. He did not steal, did not kill, did not conspire. The charge is different: he asks too many questions. He corrupts the young by teaching them to question. He disrespects the city's gods by doubting the certainties everyone pretends to hold.

His name is Socrates.

He could have escaped. The judges offered him a way out: stop asking questions and leave. He refused. He said something that was recorded for all time, in the words of the disciple who accompanied him to the end: "An unexamined life is not worth living."

He was condemned by 280 votes to 220. He drank hemlock (a poison made from a plant that paralyzes the body progressively until the heart stops). He died a few hours later, surrounded by friends.

Socrates's real crime was simple: he did not know, and he said so in public. More than that, he proved that others did not know either, even though they pretended to. Generals who claimed to understand courage could not define it when he asked. Politicians who claimed to understand justice froze when he pressed for examples. Craftsmen who mastered their trade believed they mastered everything, and Socrates showed, patiently, that they did not.

The phrase that made him immortal — "I know that I know nothing" — was not empty humility. It was a method: starting from not-knowing in order to truly learn, instead of starting from certainty and defending what you already believe.

Athens did not forgive him for it. The city preferred the death of the questioner to the death of its certainties.

Two thousand and three hundred years later, a young Austrian from a Jewish family read the story of Socrates in a Vienna that was beginning to burn. He was watching the same mechanism live: societies that punish doubt. That elect absolute certainties and kill those who disagree — sometimes literally.

That young man's name was Karl Popper.

Socrates paid with his life for asking too many questions. Popper spent his entire life trying to understand why societies do this and how to build systems that do not.

The answer he found is the central idea of this article.

**Why does Popper begin here?**

Socrates was not naive. He knew that doubt is uncomfortable. That those who question threaten those with an interest in keeping the answers ready. The Socratic method — the name given to his way of asking questions until contradictions are revealed — is not a debating technique. It is an architecture of discovery: you do not reach the truth by proclamation, you reach it by eliminating error.

Popper took that intuition and turned it into a system. If Socrates showed that other people's certainties were false, Popper asked: how do you build a world where falsity can be discovered and corrected without the one who discovers it being killed?

The answer has a name: open society. And it begins exactly where the execution of Socrates ends — in the recognition that no idea, no law, no system is so sacred that it cannot be questioned.

## **1. Where it all begins.**

Christchurch, New Zealand, February 1943. A city of immaculate gardens and British silence at the end of the world.

In a cramped office at the University of Canterbury, a forty-year-old Austrian types furiously. He has no secretary. His wife, Hennie, types the manuscripts in her spare time. The university told him, in so many words, that research was "theft of working time." Publishing? Out of the question. He published anyway.

Karl Popper had fled Vienna five years earlier. Jewish family, Austria swallowed by the Reich in 1938. He secured a post in the most distant place that agreed to take him. No decent library. No colleagues. He even translated Plato from Greek on his own because he could not find the edition he needed.

On the other side of the globe, Europe was burning. Extermination camps running at industrial scale. Popper followed it all on the radio, months delayed, and two questions gnawed at him: how did Nazism happen? How do you prevent it from happening again?

In April 1943, he finished the manuscript. Six hundred pages. He sent it to England by sea mail. The book was rejected by several publishers. It was Friedrich Hayek — Austrian economist, exiled in London, who will appear in the next article — who found a publisher willing to print it.

In November 1945, the book came out: *The Open Society and Its Enemies*. Academics hated it. Popper attacked Plato, Hegel, and Marx. Heresy. But historian Michael King wrote that it was "the most influential book ever written in New Zealand."

## **2. Historical context.**

The year is 1945. The Second World War left between 70 and 85 million dead. Two atomic bombs in Japan. Europe in rubble. The Holocaust: six million Jews, plus millions of Roma, homosexuals, and people with disabilities.

The uncomfortable question: how did entire societies accept systems that demanded blind obedience? The dominant answer: evil leaders deceived innocent peoples. Popper disagreed. The problem was older. Plato, Hegel, and Marx had built justifications for societies where criticism is a crime and obedience is a virtue. The twentieth-century dictators did not invent totalitarianism. They inherited a tradition that had already paved the road.

## **3. The central idea.**

Popper's thesis fits in two sentences.

An open society is not one that is always right. It is one that allows errors to be identified and corrected without violence.

Think about your apartment building. In one version, the building manager decides everything alone, never gives an account, and anyone who complains is called "divisive." In another, any resident can flag a problem, propose a change, and, if it fails, undo it at the next meeting. The first seems efficient. The second seems messy. But which one do you prefer when the manager makes a mistake?

Before Popper, the goal of a good society was to find the truth — the perfect constitution, the ideal leader. After Popper, the goal changed: what matters is whether the system allows you to discover you were wrong and correct course in time.

Popper called this fallibilism: all human knowledge is provisional. The question that separates civilization from barbarism is not "who is right?" It is: "When someone is wrong, can the system correct it without violence?"

In science, Popper coined a rule that became the standard filter: a theory that cannot be proven wrong is not science — it is dogma. A single black swan is enough to bring down the theory that all swans are white. Good theories are ones that offer themselves to be destroyed.

In politics, the same logic. Democracy is not the best system because it produces the best leaders. It is the best because it allows replacing bad leaders without civil war.

## **4. Developments.**

### **The three enemies no one expected.**

Popper did something no sensible academic would do: he attacked Plato, Hegel, and Marx in the same book. He called all three "intellectual enemies of the open society."

Plato designed a republic governed by philosopher-kings who knew the truth and therefore could not be questioned. Popper translated: a closed system where the ruler's error has no correction mechanism.

Hegel argued that History (with a capital H) moves toward a pre-determined goal, and that the State is the embodiment of that goal. Popper translated: whoever holds power can claim to represent the inevitable future — and anyone who opposes it is opposing History itself.

Marx said that the class struggle would inevitably lead to a workers' state, after which history would end. Popper translated: a theory that cannot be falsified. Everything confirms it, nothing can refute it. By his own criterion, not science — religion.

The three share a common structure: certainty about where history is going, concentration of power at the top, and systematic elimination of the correction mechanism. They call it ideology. Popper called it the architecture of disasters.

### **Piecemeal social engineering.**

Popper's alternative was not utopia-building. It was something modest and uncomfortable: piecemeal social engineering. Instead of planning the perfect society and imposing it by force, you make small changes, observe what happens, correct what went wrong, and move on. Each change is reversible. No bet requires burning everything before you can start.

Popper was not against change. He was against irreversible changes based on absolute certainties.

### **The paradox of tolerance.**

Popper answered a thorny question: should an open society tolerate the intolerant? His answer: no, if they threaten to destroy the openness. A tolerant society that tolerates the intolerant without limit will be destroyed by them. The Nazis used the freedom of expression of the Weimar Republic to destroy Weimar. Openness has to protect itself from those who want to close it. That is not censorship of uncomfortable opinions. It is the recognition that freedom of expression does not include the freedom to destroy freedom of expression.

## **5. Historical legacy.**

George Soros read Popper at the London School of Economics in the 1950s. Decades later, he created the Open Society Foundations — the name comes directly from the book — with a budget of billions, dedicated to promoting open societies in Eastern Europe.

In the 1980s and 1990s, when Poland, Hungary, and Czechoslovakia emerged from decades of communism, *The Open Society* became a handbook for democratic reconstruction. Václav Havel, the playwright who became Czech president, cited Popper as a reference. Charter 77, a manifesto by Czech dissidents, carried Popperian logic without mentioning the name: the right to criticize power precedes any constitution.

In science, the falsifiability criterion became the standard filter. A doctor who says "my treatment works, but it cannot be tested"? Popper would say: that is not medicine. That is belief.

## **6. Contradictions and limitations.**

Popper attacked Plato with disproportionate vigor. Many scholars argue that he oversimplified *The Republic*, ignored the Athenian context, and treated philosophical dialogues as a political manifesto. The critique of Hegel is also contested: Hegelian philosophers argue that Popper distorted complex concepts to fit his narrative. This is not academic consensus. It is one side's thesis.

There is another, more subtle contradiction. Popper preached piecemeal engineering, but presented his own ideas with the certainty of someone who accepts no challenge. Students at the London School of Economics reported that he was intolerant of disagreement in seminars. The defender of the open society was, in practice, an autocratic debater. Even great ideas have limits. Popper's limit was Popper.

## **7. The translation into versioning.**

Popper died in 1994. He never used email. He had never heard of Git. But his ideas describe with precision what Torvalds built in code a decade later.

An objection comes up here often: fallibilism is a scientific idea. What does it have to do with a board meeting, a cooperative assembly, a city council vote? The answer is in the pattern, not the vocabulary. Every group that makes decisions without a structured criticism mechanism is repeating Plato's mistake: assuming that whoever decides knows more and therefore does not need to be questioned. The criterion changes. The risk is the same.

**Fallibilism is commit.** The commit — the record of a change to the system — starts from the premise that every decision is provisional. You record what you did, with date, author, and reason. Not because it is perfect. Because it needs to be traceable for when you discover you were wrong.

**Step 12 — Review:** Popper said no one is immune to error. In the 21-Step system, review makes this structural: every proposal passes through another pair of eyes before being incorporated. It is not personal distrust. It is recognition that the human brain has blind spots the person cannot see in themselves. Whoever proposes does not approve. Whoever approves is not who will execute alone. The separation is not bureaucracy: it is the Popperian correction mechanism, before the error becomes a consequence.

**Open society is open repository.** Anyone can see the code, point out a bug, propose a fix. In a closed system, errors stay hidden until collapse. Raymond said: "Given enough eyes, all bugs are shallow." Popper would say: "Given enough criticism, all errors are correctable." Same logic.

**Piecemeal engineering is branch, test, merge, revert.** You do not bring down the whole system to test an idea. You create a separate draft (branch), test it, and only incorporate it into the official version (merge) if it works. If it fails, you undo it (revert). Change that is reversible, incremental, testable.

**The paradox of tolerance is repository protection.** If anyone can merge directly without review, someone will inject malicious code. Total openness without protection destroys itself. That is why mandatory review exists.

But Popper's warning applies here too: if the maintainers start treating their decisions as infallible, if review becomes an empty ritual, if fork is treated as betrayal — the repository has become a closed society.

## **8. Practical implication.**

In 1960, an American pharmaceutical company applied for approval to sell thalidomide in the United States — the same drug already causing birth defects in Europe. Frances Kelsey, a physician recently hired by the FDA (the American agency that evaluates drug safety), refused to approve it. Not because she had definitive proof of harm. Because the data presented was not falsifiable: the company had not demonstrated in a testable way that the drug was safe for pregnant women.

Kelsey held firm for 19 months against pressure from industry and superiors. In that time, more than 10,000 children were born with deformities in Europe. In the United States, the number stayed below 20. The system had, in that instance, a Popperian mechanism: approval required the applicant to demonstrate safety in a verifiable way, not merely declare it. In 1962, Kelsey received the highest civilian honor in the United States. Popper would have said the system did not work because Kelsey was smarter. It worked because the criterion was more rigorous: the burden of proof lay with whoever made the claim, not with whoever doubted it.

An illustrative example: a farming cooperative in the Brazilian interior votes to accept a new supplier. Six months later, the supplier falls behind on everything, large losses, and no one knows who proposed it, who argued in favor, or what the terms were. Consensus without a record is Popper's closed society in miniature.

If the same cooperative had written up the proposal (issue), recorded the arguments (review), and documented the decision with date and names (commit), the error would be traceable. And reversible.

## **9. Lessons learned.**

Good systems do not always get it right. They correct quickly.

Anyone who claims absolute certainty is asking you to stop thinking.

Test small, observe, correct, repeat. Total revolution is a bet made with other people's money.

Openness without protection destroys itself. Every open system needs rules against those who want to close it.

An error without a record is an error that repeats. A recorded error is an error that teaches.

## **10. Exploring paths and connections.**

In the previous article, Locke taught us that no authority is legitimate without consent.

Popper took the next step: consent is not enough. What if the majority votes for catastrophe? The value of a system is not being right. It is being able to correct itself.

Locke said where authority comes from: bottom up. Popper said what to do when authority makes a mistake: correct quickly, without violence, with a record.

But a question remains. Correcting errors requires information. Who has that information? The president? The central committee?

In 1945, the same year Popper published *The Open Society*, an Austrian economist — another exile — published a twenty-page article. Friedrich Hayek. The thesis: no person, no committee can gather the information needed to make good decisions. Useful knowledge is scattered across millions of minds.

If Popper showed that erring is inevitable, Hayek will show that centralizing the correction is impossible.
