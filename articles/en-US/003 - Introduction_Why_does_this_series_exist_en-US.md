# **Governance as Versioning**

## **Introduction: Why does this series exist?**

---

**What will you find here?**

Three origin stories (the Xerox printer, the Finnish student in his room, the tool built in ten days) and the question that connects all of them: if it worked for code, why wouldn't it work for human decisions?

---

### **1. The invisible revealed**

Right now, as you read these words, something invisible sustains almost everything you do. Your phone works because there is an operating system called Linux running on the servers that deliver your messages, your videos, your payments. Linux is a computer program created by volunteers from around the world, with no owner, no company behind it, no boss. Anyone can see how it works on the inside, modify whatever they want, distribute their own version. That is called open source (or free software): a program with no lock on it.

Linux runs on 90% of the internet's servers. It runs on Android smartphones. On NASA's supercomputers. On the websites you use most: social networks, banks, email services, streaming platforms. On stock exchanges. On aircraft navigation systems. It is the largest voluntary collaboration in human history.

And almost no one knows it exists.

The way thousands of developers work together on this software, without ever having met, uses a tool called Git: a system that records every change made by every person, with a date, an author, and a reason. If someone makes a mistake, the mistake can be undone without destroying the rest. If someone disagrees with the direction, they can make a copy and go their own way. Everything open. Everything traceable. Everything reversible.

But there is something even more invisible than Linux and Git. And far more urgent.

Our systems of governance — assemblies, councils, boards, associations, cooperatives, homeowner associations — still operate on rules from centuries ago. Rules designed when information traveled by horse, not fiber optic cable. When a letter from Paris to Lyon took days. When the world was slow enough to wait.

We are trying to run a 2026 application on a computer from the 1980s. It freezes. It overheats. It cannot keep up.

### **2. Why does this series exist?**

There is an old tradition of translating complicated ideas into language that anyone can understand. Voltaire used short stories to criticize power and superstition. Montesquieu pretended to be a Persian visiting France to expose absurdities that the French could no longer see. Herodotus told stories of distant peoples to teach about his own.

This series wants to do something similar: translate complexity.

The thesis is straightforward. Polarization, corruption, misinformation, low representation, conflicts of interest, management that borders on incompetence, laws that no one understands, and even lawfare (the strategic and abusive use of the legal system as a political weapon, simulating legality): all of these are symptoms. The problem is architectural. Structural. Like the foundation of a house: if the base is crooked, repainting the walls or replacing the doorknobs changes nothing. You have to work on the foundation.

To understand anything deeply, it helps to know where it came from. Someone who woke up in the middle of a road with no idea where they started can walk in any direction, but almost none of them will be right. Without a trajectory, there is no orientation. This series follows a historical and chronological logic for that reason: first, the context in which each idea was born. Then, how it evolved, what was corrected, what survived the test of time. The method that organizes this has a name: LATCH, proposed by Richard Saul Wurman. Five ways to structure any information: Location, Alphabet, Time, Category, Hierarchy. This series uses time to reveal causality, hierarchy to show who influenced whom, and category to reveal patterns that only appear when you compare ideas from different centuries. This is not methodological decoration. It is what makes it possible to see invisible structures across 335 years of thought.

What free software discovered in four decades can help fix that foundation. Not because technology solves everything (it does not), but because the lessons of how thousands of people work together without a boss apply to any human group that needs to make collective decisions.

### **3. The three origin stories**

**The printer that could not be fixed**

MIT, 1980. Richard Stallman was a programmer at the Artificial Intelligence Laboratory. Long hair, unkempt beard, worn-out sneakers. The lab's printer jammed constantly. Everyone complained. Stallman had a simple idea: modify the printer's program so it would automatically alert users when it jammed. Problem solved, right?

Wrong. Xerox, the printer's manufacturer, refused to provide the source code — the internal instructions that make the program work. Trade secret. Closed. Locked. Stallman went to a colleague at another university who had access to the code and asked for a copy. The colleague, who had signed a nondisclosure agreement with Xerox, refused.

Stallman was furious. Not about the printer. About the principle: someone in a distant office had decided he could not fix his own work tool. Someone had put a lock on knowledge.

In 1983, he launched the GNU Project. In 1985, he founded the Free Software Foundation and wrote the GPL (General Public License): the first license to guarantee four freedoms by law. To use the program for any purpose. To study how it works and modify it. To redistribute copies. To distribute modified versions.

Stallman did not invent technology. He invented ethics for technology. He said: knowledge should not have a lock on it.

**The student who built an operating system in his room**

Helsinki, 1991. Linus Torvalds was 21 years old and studying Computer Science. He wanted to use Unix (a professional operating system) on the university's computers, but access was limited. Controlled hours. Endless queues. For a student with no money in Finland, buying Unix was impossible: it cost thousands of dollars.

Instead of complaining, he built his own. Alone. In his room. As a hobby.

On August 25th, he posted a message on an online forum: "I'm doing a free operating system. Just a hobby, won't be big and professional." He released the source code as open. Anyone could see it, modify it, improve it. He called it Linux.

What happened surprised even Torvalds. Developers from around the world began to contribute. They fixed bugs. Added features. Improved performance. No boss. No company. No salary. Simply because they wanted to and they could.

In a few years, Linux became the most reliable operating system on the planet. Torvalds proved that you do not need a billion-dollar corporation, an army of managers, and nondisclosure contracts to create something extraordinary. You only need motivated people, clear rules, and total openness.

**The tool built in ten days**

Early 2000s. Linux was already enormous. Thousands of developers collaborating. Torvalds used a tool called BitKeeper to manage changes to the code. BitKeeper was proprietary software, but its owner allowed the Linux community to use it for free, with one condition: no one could try to reverse-engineer how it worked.

In 2005, a developer violated that condition. The owner of BitKeeper cancelled the free access. No warning. No negotiation. Overnight, Torvalds and the entire Linux community had no tool and years of development history at risk.

Torvalds was furious. Again. And again, instead of complaining, he acted. In ten days, he created Git: a system for managing versions of code that was distributed (no single entity controlled it), fast, and completely open.

The lesson repeated itself: when someone locks access, the answer is not to beg for permission. It is to build something that no one can lock again.

Raymond had already written, in 1997, an essay that changed how the world understood free software: "The Cathedral and the Bazaar." In the cathedral, a small group designs everything in secret and delivers it finished. In the bazaar, anyone can contribute, review, improve. The cathedral is beautiful but fragile. The bazaar is noisy but finds errors fast. Raymond coined the phrase that became a rule: "Given enough eyes, all bugs are shallow."

Git and Linux were living proof that Raymond was right.

### **4. What unites the three**

Three men. Three frustrations. Three responses that changed the world without asking anyone's permission.

Stallman faced a locked printer and created a philosophy of freedom. Torvalds faced denied access twice (to the university and to BitKeeper) and created Linux and Git. Raymond observed organized chaos and explained why it worked better than planned order.

The pattern is always the same: someone tries to lock knowledge. The answer is not to beg for access. It is to build something open that no one can lock again.

Frustration as fuel. Openness as the answer. Leaderless collaboration as the method.

And it worked. Not just worked: it surpassed everything that came before. The entire digital world runs on what these three built. Your phone, your favorite websites, Netflix, WhatsApp, your bank. All of it running on Linux servers. All the software code that makes it possible versioned with tools that follow the principles Git established. All open source.

Almost no one knows. But now you do.

### **5. Why this matters to you**

You might be thinking: fine for software. But what does this have to do with my life, my homeowner association, my cooperative, my city?

Everything.

If thousands of volunteers create software more reliable than billion-dollar corporations, why can't citizens create better rules than professional politicians?

If Git allows every change, every decision, every mistake to be tracked with full transparency, why don't our organizations use that principle?

If open source has proved that openness is not weakness (it is a correction mechanism), why do we keep accepting that collective decisions are made behind closed doors?

The answer is simple: because no one has put the pieces together. Yet.

### **6. Series structure and what comes next**

This series has 21 core articles, plus a preface, introduction, conclusion, and afterword. This text you just read is the introduction: the foundation on which everything else rests. After it come 21 articles, each dedicated to a thinker who adds a piece to the puzzle. And a conclusion that opens the door to what follows.

In total, 29 authors across 335 years: from John Locke in 1689 to Ethan Mollick in 2024. Philosophers, economists, biologists, mathematicians, sociologists. Each piece connects to the one before and the one after.

The series will be published in Portuguese, English, and Spanish. Then French, Italian, German. Anyone who wants to help translate is invited. The bazaar model applied to the project itself.

Now let us go back 335 years.

England, 1689. A philosopher named John Locke has just returned from exile in the Netherlands. The king who persecuted him has fled the country. And Locke publishes, anonymously, a book that says something no one had dared to put on paper before: no government is legitimate if the people have not consented to it.

It sounds obvious today. In 1689, it was the kind of sentence that could cost the writer their head.

Locke did not know it, but he had just invented the first revert in political history.
