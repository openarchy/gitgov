# **The Original Revert**

## **Article 01 of 21 — Governance as Versioning series**

---

*This is Article 01 of the series. It covers John Locke (1689, Two Treatises of Government), whose central concept is consent as the source of legitimate authority — the political foundation of the revert mechanism. The corresponding step in the 21 Steps is Step 15 (Revert).*

---

**What you'll find here.**

Have you ever been part of a decision that couldn't be undone? An assembly that approved something by majority vote, with no mechanism whatsoever to correct it if things went wrong? A board that changed the rules mid-game and nobody knew how to contest it?

This problem is 335 years old. And someone already proposed the solution.

---

## **1. Where it all begins**

London, winter of 1689. King James II had just fled to France. Nobody wept.

For years, he governed as an absolute monarch. Ignored Parliament. Persecuted Protestants. Appointed allies to positions of power. When the people complained, the answer was silence or the sword.

William of Orange, a Dutchman married to James's own daughter, crossed the Channel with an army. He didn't need to fight. The king fled in the early hours of the morning, throwing the Great Seal of England into the Thames (as if erasing the stamp could erase the failure). Historians call it the Glorious Revolution: a change of government almost without bloodshed.

But something hung in the air. A philosophical gap the size of a continent.

If kings govern because God chose them (that's what everyone believed), why did God let this one be expelled? And if the new king was also chosen by God, why did he need an army?

Nobody had an answer. Except a 57-year-old man, freshly returned from exile in Holland, discreet, methodical, dangerous in his ideas. John Locke opened his trunk, pulled out a manuscript, and changed the history of humanity.

The book was called *Two Treatises of Government*. And the thesis was a bomb: governments don't fall from the sky. They are created by people. And people can undo them.

---

## **2. Historical context**

In 1689, all of Europe ran on "those with power command, those with sense obey." Absolute monarchies in France, Spain, Portugal, Russia. Kings owned the country: the land, the laws, the people. The justification? Divine right. God chose the king. To question the king was to question God.

Robert Filmer, an English intellectual, had published *Patriarcha* years earlier: kings are like fathers. Subjects are like children. Children obey. Full stop. The idea was so accepted that no one found it strange.

Locke did. And he decided it was time to say why.

He wasn't a barricade revolutionary. He was a physician, philosopher, political advisor. He had lived in the shadow of power and seen from close range how it worked. He knew that wrong ideas sustain entire systems.

---

## **3. The central idea**

Locke's thesis fits in one sentence: no authority is legitimate if people have not consented to it.

It seems obvious today. In 1689, it was asking to be hanged.

Locke asked us to imagine a world without government. He called it the "state of nature." In that state, everyone is free and equal. Nobody is born superior. There are no kings, no judges, no written laws.

But there is a problem: you plant wheat, work for months, harvest, store it. Someone stronger appears and takes everything. You try to defend yourself, but you're alone. In the state of nature, everyone is judge in their own cause. Result: endless conflicts, permanent insecurity.

What would rational people do? Make an agreement. Each person gives up part of their freedom (like taking justice into their own hands) in exchange for collective security (a government that protects, arbitrates, punishes). Locke called this the social contract.

The contract is not a real document. Nobody signed it. It is an implicit agreement: you accept living in a society with government? Then you are agreeing to the rules.

But the government doesn't get a blank check. Power flows from the bottom up. People cede authority on one condition: that the government protects life, liberty, and property. If it fails, if it begins to oppress instead of protect, the contract is broken. And the people have the right to replace the government.

Before Locke, the logic was "obey because God willed it." After Locke, the logic became "obey as long as it serves." The difference between those two sentences is the difference between a subject and a citizen.

## **4. Developments**

### **Labor as the birth certificate of property**

Before Locke, property came from conquest (you kill someone and keep the land), inheritance (your grandfather was a noble), or a gift from the king. Locke inverted this: property comes from labor.

Imagine a forest with no owner. You enter, cut timber, build a house. The house is yours. Why? Because you mixed your effort with raw matter. You plant, harvest, transform. The product is yours.

In 1689, this was dynamite. It meant that a peasant who worked the land had more right to it than a lazy noble who inherited a title. It meant that labor generates legitimacy, not blood or decree.

This idea has a direct echo in open source: your contribution (commit) is yours. Nobody can erase your name from the history. Merit is proven by what you did, not by the position you hold.

### **Divide to prevent corruption**

Locke noticed a pattern: those who concentrate power abuse it. Always. Not necessarily out of malice, but because the temptation is too great.

The solution: divide. Locke proposed separating those who make the laws (Legislative) from those who execute (Executive) from those who handle foreign affairs (Federative). Nobody can be judge in their own cause. If the king makes the laws, executes them, and judges those who break them, he is a tyrant by definition.

Montesquieu would refine this later, adding the Judiciary. But the seed is Locke's: concentration is danger, distribution is protection. This logic appears intact in the 21 Steps: nobody approves their own work (Step 12). Always another pair of eyes.

## **5. Historical legacy**

Thomas Jefferson, when writing the Declaration of Independence of the United States in 1776, practically copied Locke: "We hold these truths to be self-evident: that all men are created equal, endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights, among which are Life, Liberty and the pursuit of Happiness." Jefferson swapped "property" for "the pursuit of Happiness." The rest is Locke, word for word.

The French Revolution (1789) drew from the same source. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen echoes the social contract, consent, and the limits of power.

Constitutions around the world carry Locke's fingerprints: separation of powers, popular sovereignty, rights that exist before any law. Brazil, Portugal, Germany, South Africa, India. Locke didn't invent democracy, but he gave it its modern justification.

---

## **6. Contradictions and limitations**

Locke defended liberty, equality, natural rights. But he invested in the Royal African Company, which trafficked enslaved people. He helped write the Constitutions of Carolina (an American colony), which permitted slavery.

"All men are born free and equal" and at the same time profiting from slavery. He never resolved that contradiction. His ideas were used both to liberate (abolition, independence movements) and to oppress (colonialism, dispossession of indigenous peoples).

This teaches something the series itself defends: good ideas don't guarantee good practice. The system requires constant review, including (and especially) of whoever created it. Had Locke been subjected to peer review with the diversity his time lacked, the contradiction would have surfaced sooner.

## **7. The translation into versioning**

Locke never saw a computer. He died in 1704, three centuries before Git existed. But his ideas explain the heart of versioning as if he had read the manual.

Before the parallels: a legitimate objection. This works in software, where rules are code and history is automatic. Governance is made of people, politics, interests. How does a system designed for text files solve that?

The answer is in the mechanism, not the medium. What Git does with files, the 21 Steps do with decisions: they record who proposed, when, with what justification. They make history visible. They allow undoing. The technology changes. The principle is the same one Locke described in 1689.

**Rebellion is revert.** Locke said: if the government breaks the contract, the people can undo it. It's not chaos, not anarchy. It's a legitimate correction mechanism. In Git, revert (Step 15) does exactly that: it undoes a specific decision that went wrong, without destroying the history. Locke invented political revert 316 years before Torvalds invented technical revert. This article has the name it has for that reason.

**Step 15 — Revert:** Locke said that power without an undo mechanism is tyranny. In the 21 Steps system, revert is that mechanism: you undo a specific decision without erasing the history of what happened. Consent is not final; it is ongoing. When the agreement fails, there is a way back. Legitimacy is rebuilt through process, not decree.

**Consent is opt-in, not imposition.** In Git, nobody is forced to accept a merge. In Locke's social contract, nobody is forced to accept a government that doesn't represent them. Consent is the foundation: without it, the system is illegitimate. In versioning, this appears in Steps 1 to 4 (the four freedoms): to see, to participate, to adapt, to share. If any one of them is blocked, there is no real consent.

**Separation of powers is mandatory review.** Locke said: nobody judges themselves. In versioning, Step 12 says the same: every decision requires someone else to check before it is accepted. Nobody approves their own work. The principle is identical: concentration of power corrupts; review distributes.

**Property through labor is authorship through commit.** Locke said: what you produced with your effort is yours. In Git, every commit records the author, the date, the reason. Your contribution is inscribed. Merit is proven by the public history, not by title or appointment.

Vigilance alert: even open systems can betray Locke. If a small group controls who can merge, who can open an issue, who has permission to fork, the consent of the governed has become fiction. The system looks open but operates like an absolute monarchy with a nice interface.

## **8. Practical implication**

In June 1215, a group of English barons did something unprecedented: they forced King John to sign a document limiting his own power. Magna Carta was not a modern constitution. It was a contract of 63 clauses that said, in plain language: there are things the king cannot do. Imprison someone without trial. Levy taxes without approval. Ignore the law he himself had promised to follow.

What the barons invented in 1215 is the mechanism Locke would describe 474 years later in philosophical language: power has conditions. If the conditions are broken, the agreement can be undone. King John signed, ignored the document, the barons reacted, and Magna Carta had to be reissued several times before becoming permanent law. The history was recorded. The possibility of reverting arbitrary power came to exist as a principle, not just as rebellion. The first documented political revert in Western history happened at the field of Runnymede, on a June afternoon, 474 years before Locke wrote the theory behind it.

Now imagine a neighborhood association that decides everything at in-person meetings, on Tuesdays, at 7 PM. Those who work nights never participate. Those who live far away, the same. Decisions are made by whoever shows up, not by whoever is affected.

If that association adopted Locke's principle (consent of the governed), it would need to ensure that everyone could see the decisions (Step 1), propose changes (Step 9), and contest bad decisions (Step 15). The in-person meeting doesn't disappear, but it stops being the only channel.

## **9. Lessons learned**

Consent is not a single vote: it is ongoing permission. If it stops serving, it can be revoked.

No authority is legitimate by decree. Legitimacy is built and maintained through accountability.

Separating those who decide from those who execute from those who oversee is not bureaucracy: it is protection against abuse.

Property (or authorship) comes from visible labor, not from position or title. A public history speaks louder than any credential.

Even the creator of a system can contradict their own principles. The system must function despite the failures of whoever created it.

## **10. Exploring paths and connections**

This is the first article in the core of the *Governance as Versioning* series. The introduction presented Stallman, Raymond, and Torvalds: the tools. Now we begin to dig the foundations: why do those tools work?

Locke answered the first question: where does authority come from? From consent. Power that flows from the top down, without permission, is tyranny. Power that flows from the bottom up, with conditions, is legitimate government. And legitimate government can be undone when it fails.

But Locke left an enormous gap.

What if the people consent to something terrible? What if the majority, of its own free will, approves a disastrous law? What if a system based on consent produces terrible results?

Locke had no answer for that. But in 1945, an Austrian philosopher taking refuge in London during the Second World War wrote a book that does: the value of a system is not in getting it right. It is in the speed with which it can correct.

His name was Karl Popper. And he will show us that the right question is not "who should govern?" but "how do we get rid of bad rulers without bloodshed?"
