# You Never Start from Zero
## How to use what you already know to learn anything faster

*Article Alpha — Governance as Versioning series*

*This is the Alpha Article. It's not the first in the series — it's what comes before the first. The Governance as Versioning series officially begins with the Zero Article, published on April 7th, when Git turns 21. Alpha exists because any honest argument shows the method before applying it. What you'll read here is the foundation for everything that follows: the idea that structures repeat across different fields, and that seeing this changes the speed at which you learn anything. The entire series depends on that recognition.*

---

**What you'll find here**

You're about to learn something nobody formally teaches. And it changes the speed at which you learn anything from this point forward.

Most people, when facing something new, do the same thing: they try to start from zero. They discard what they know, look for the right course, wait for the perfect moment. And take twice as long as necessary.

The problem isn't lack of effort. It's lack of method. And the method is simple: you never start from zero. You start from everything you've lived, learned, and forgotten you know.

I didn't learn this from a book. Life forced it on me.

---

I'm three years old. I'm in kindergarten in Buenos Aires, learning English because my parents thought it would be good for me. Nobody asked my opinion.

Just before starting primary school, around five or six, I fall off a skateboard and break my right arm. The cast lasts months. My teacher had no choice: she taught me to write with my left hand. I had no choice either: I learned. When the cast came off, I kept using both hands. Not out of talent. Out of necessity.

That was the first time I understood — without having words for it — that learning isn't starting from zero. It's finding the map that already exists and drawing a new path on it.

I didn't learn that from a book. Life made me.

We've never had so much knowledge available. Any subject, in seconds. Any format: text, video, audio, podcast, online course, AI summary in 30 seconds. The distance between "I know nothing about X" and "I have access to everything about X" has never been shorter.

And at the same time, more people than ever feel stuck when trying to learn something new.

That's not a coincidence. It's the same problem with two faces. More access without method becomes noise. AI accelerates both sides: those who have the method learn faster than was ever possible. Those who don't drown at higher speed.

The good news: the method exists. And it doesn't start from zero.

Tomorrow morning, someone will walk into a meeting and propose an idea that has existed for 300 years. Under a different name. In a different field. Dressed differently.

They'll propose it again because they didn't recognize the shape. Because they thought they were starting from zero.

They weren't.

That idea — that the outsider's view reveals what the insider's view cannot see — has at least three centuries of documented use. In 1721, Montesquieu published *Persian Letters*: an epistolary novel in which two fictional Persians, Usbek and Rica, visit France and write letters home describing what they see. What they see, of course, is what the French couldn't see in themselves: the absurdity of the court, the irrationality of the laws, the vanity of customs. Montesquieu was French. But he needed a foreigner to say what a Frenchman couldn't say without being arrested.

The device was deliberate. He called it the "method of estrangement": the condition of knowledge is not belonging to the society being described. Those too far inside cannot see. Those far enough outside see what was invisible.

Three hundred years later, it's still true.

There's a pattern that separates fast learners from those who always restart: recognizing that shapes repeat, under different names, in apparently unrelated fields. Those who see this stop restarting. They start reusing.

## 2. What the bicycle teaches about learning

I was eight when my family moved to Belgium. From Spanish to French and Flemish at the same time — two languages with nothing in common. There was no elegant method. There was school on Monday morning and the need to understand what was happening.

What I noticed, stumbling through it: French and Spanish have the same skeleton. Verb conjugations follow the same rhythm. Prepositions fall in the same places. The map I already had from Spanish didn't solve everything, but it solved far more than I expected.

But language isn't just grammar. I learned that the hard way.

Every culture has symbols, rituals, and values that work like a password for entry. In Belgium, there was a right way to greet, an expected way to behave at the table, a tone of voice for the classroom, gestures that meant respect and gestures that meant arrogance. Nobody explains this. You observe, test, fail, adjust. Those who only learn grammar learn the language. Those who learn the symbols and rituals learn the culture. They're two different maps, and both are necessary.

This has a technical name. And a much simpler analogy as well.

I imagine you've learned to ride a bicycle. If you tried a motorcycle for the first time, you didn't start from zero. Dynamic balance, progressive braking, wide turns, anticipatory vision: it was all there. Your brain didn't restart. It found the old map and adapted. The only new element was the engine.

This happens with everything you learn. The question is knowing how to recognize when the map already exists inside you.

AI can greatly accelerate this process: it finds patterns, explains connections, translates languages between fields. But it can only do this well if you know what you're looking for. A tool without method is noise with a pretty interface.

## 3. The ugly word for a simple idea

Isomorphism. *Iso* means equal. *Morphe* means form. Two things that look different on the outside but have the same architecture on the inside.

A subway map and a geographic map look completely different. The drawing changes, the distances change. But the connections between stations are the same. The structure is preserved.

A musical score and a computer program look like opposite worlds. One is for musicians, the other for machines. But both do the same thing: they store instructions in sequence to produce a result.

For mathematics, a coffee mug and a donut are the same thing: each has exactly one hole. If you could knead them like modeling clay, one would become the other without tearing or gluing. That third example is the strangest, and deliberately so: isomorphism doesn't respect what seems obvious.

Charles Berlitz spent decades doing exactly this with languages. In *The World of Language* and in his conversation guide series, he didn't teach vocabulary: he dismantled architectures. He showed where English and Japanese diverge in sentence structure, where Arabic and Hebrew share common roots, where Portuguese and Italian are almost the same map with different names. Berlitz made the reader see languages as systems, not as word lists. I learned a great deal from him. It's isomorphism applied to linguistics, before the word existed.

Mikhail Petrunin took this a step further. In *Comparative Grammar of Spanish, Portuguese, Italian and French* (2024), he places the four languages side by side on the same page, in parallel columns. You don't alternate between books: you see simultaneously where Spanish says "hablo", Portuguese says "falo", Italian says "parlo" and French says "je parle". The shared structure appears in the same visual field, without requiring memory or comparison effort. The reader doesn't learn four languages from zero: they recognize an architecture they already partially know, dressed in four different outfits. It's the principle of this article transformed into a presentation method: you never start from zero because the structure connecting the languages was there before you opened the book.

## 4. The four types that work in real life

I was almost twelve or thirteen when we moved to Peru. I had to relearn the Spanish I thought I knew. Argentine Spanish and Peruvian Spanish have the same skeleton, but different rhythms, different vocabulary, completely distinct forms of courtesy. I spoke incorrectly for months. Sometimes amusingly. Sometimes embarrassingly.

At almost eighteen, I arrived in Brazil. Portuguese wasn't the biggest obstacle. The biggest was methodological: French schooling works one way — argument, structure, formal defense of a thesis. Brazilian university works differently — relationship with the professor, participation, context, informality in unexpected places. Two completely different teaching architectures.

What I realized, more slowly than I should have: I wasn't starting from zero at any of those moments. I was identifying what type of isomorphism existed between the systems, and where it broke down.

There are four types that work in practice.

**Structural** preserves who connects with whom. An organizational chart, the nervous system, and the internet have the same network form. When you understand one, you're halfway to the others.

**Functional** preserves what the system does, not how it works internally. The heart and a water pump perform the same function: pushing liquid in cycles. Engineers and doctors study similar problems with different names.

Here's a pop example that illustrates this better than any manual. In *Pygmalion*, George Bernard Shaw's 1913 play — which you probably know better as the musical *My Fair Lady* — phonetics professor Henry Higgins makes a bet: that he can transform Eliza Doolittle, a flower seller with a Cockney East End London accent, into a high-society lady, accepted in any salon. The content doesn't change: same person, same intelligence, same base vocabulary. What changes is the access code. The pronunciation, the speech rhythm, the right pauses, the expected references. Shaw called this phonetics. It's functional isomorphism: the social function is the same, the entry protocol is different.

**Dynamic** preserves how the system self-corrects. A thermostat, a goal-managed company, and a natural ecosystem function by the same logic: they act, receive feedback on what happened, and adjust. Understand one and you understand all three.

**Deep** is the rarest and most powerful. It preserves the entire organizing logic: not just the pieces, but how they talk to each other and transform. Symbols, values, and rituals of a culture are deep isomorphism. When you read them correctly, you understand why people do what they do — not just what they do.

## 5. The trap: not every similarity is real

Here's where the danger lives. Two things can look alike on the outside and be completely different inside.

"A company is like a family." Sounds true. Breaks down when you have to fire someone. Family is unconditional bond. A company is a contract. You don't fire your son for poor quarterly performance. It's an emotional analogy, not a real structure.

"The brain is like a computer." Useful for explaining memory and processing. Breaks under pressure. A computer stalls and stops. The brain degrades gradually, adapts, compensates. One is centralized, the other massively parallel. It's a useful metaphor, not a complete isomorphism.

"The state is like a company." Works in some management aspects. Breaks at the foundations. A company can fire people. A state cannot fire its citizens. Profit metrics don't apply to social stability. It's partial isomorphism, not complete.

The difference between an analogy that inspires and a structure that works is this: the one that works survives when you push it to its limits.

There's a 1986 film that illustrates this better than any academic example. In *Crocodile Dundee*, Mick Dundee arrives in New York from the Australian outback. (For readers who haven't seen it: Dundee is a wilderness survival expert dropped into Manhattan, which produces both comedy and genuine insight.) He doesn't know the city, but he knows a great deal about survival, reading environments, and assessing threats. In a luxury hotel bathroom, he finds two porcelain fixtures: one is what he knows, the other is a bidet. His conclusion: "Some idiot put two toilets in here." The object is new, but he tries to fit it into the map he has. Error of isomorphism: it looks like it, but it isn't.

In a nighttime robbery, two men threaten him with a small knife. Dundee looks at it, looks at his own, and calmly says: "That's not a knife." He draws a blade the size of a machete. "That's a knife." The muggers flee. Here the isomorphism works: a threat is a threat, and he reads the structure of the situation better than the locals, because he doesn't have the cultural filters that blind those who are too far inside.

This is what Montesquieu knew in 1721 and what the Australian comedy showed in 1986: those who come from outside, when they observe well, sometimes see what the insider can no longer see.

During the pandemic, I worked remotely with people in Canada, England, the United States, Iran, India, China, Japan, Germany, and several Latin American countries. Every culture has a different architecture for how decisions are made, how feedback is given, what silence means in a meeting. Those who tried to use the same map for everything broke quickly. Those who learned to identify where structures overlapped and where they diverged navigated better.

But there's something nobody tells you about this process. The more you transfer experiences across cultures, languages, and systems, the less rigid you become. Not because you decide to be flexible. Because you discover, in practice, that almost every system has an internal logic that makes sense to those inside it. This doesn't mean agreeing with everything. It means stopping being afraid of what is different. And when the fear decreases, curiosity increases. And when curiosity increases, empathy appears almost on its own.

I'm not saying I learned to do this well. I'm saying I learned to recognize when I was getting it wrong, and that making fewer mistakes was a consequence of caring more about understanding.

**An objection worth addressing directly**

You might be thinking: "This works for people who learned several languages or lived in other countries. My life wasn't like that."

That's a fair objection. And the answer is: you've done this too. Every time you learned a new task at work and realized it was similar to something you already did. Every time you explained a new process using a comparison with something the other person already knew. Every time you changed sectors or roles and didn't take as long to adapt as you expected.

What was missing was the name for what you were already doing intuitively. And names matter, because with one, you start doing deliberately what you previously did by accident.

## 6. How to know if a similarity is real: the 4-question test

Before using knowledge from one field to solve a problem in another, ask these four questions. If fewer than three have a clear answer, you have a pretty metaphor, not a functional structure.

1. Do the elements correspond one-to-one? Does every piece of A have an equivalent piece in B?
2. Are the relationships between them the same? Does what affects what in A also affect it in the same way in B?
3. Does the system behave the same under pressure? If you double the scale or introduce conflict, do both respond analogously?
4. Do the limits and breaking points coincide? Where A breaks, does B also break for the same reasons?

This test isn't meant to discourage. It's meant to protect. When you run it and the structure survives, you can use the knowledge with real confidence, not just hope.

A practical note: ask an AI to apply this test with you. Describe the two areas, ask the four questions, let it find where the structure holds and where it gives way. It's one of the best possible uses of the tool.

---

## 7. How to use this every day: 4 steps

**Step 1: Reduce to the minimum.** Remove the name, the jargon, the surface of each field. What are the fundamental elements? What are the relationships between them? What changes when the system is disturbed?

**Step 2: Ask, have I seen this shape before?** Not the surface: the architecture. Life cycle, network of influence, feedback system, decision hierarchy. Look for the shape, not the label. And if it's a new culture: observe the rituals, symbols, forms of respect. They say more about the structure than any manual.

**Step 3: Test under pressure.** Double the scale. Introduce conflict. Remove a central element. If the behavior in A and B diverges radically, the similarity is only apparent.

**Step 4: Transfer tools, not just language.** If the structure is real, you can move methods, predictions, and entire models — not just metaphors. That's the final criterion. If you can only transfer words, the isomorphism isn't real.

## 8. What comes next — or rather: what comes now

In a few weeks, I'll begin publishing a weekly series that applies exactly this. I'll take a system created by programmers in 2005 and use its architecture as a lens for 335 years of political philosophy, systems theory, and information science. Not as metaphor. As isomorphism: real structure, tested against the 4 questions.

But before announcing the series, I'll do what any honest argument requires: show the test working, not just describe it. A concrete demonstration with 21 technical concepts that programmers take months to absorb, presented in a story that anyone can recognize.

The system is called Git. It was created by an angry Finn over a weekend in April 2005. Linus Torvalds — the same person who created the Linux kernel — had just had the license revoked on the tool his team was using by the company that owned it. In two days he wrote an alternative from scratch. He called it Git. In the message announcing the project to the community mailing list, he described the tool with irony: "the information manager from hell."

Twenty-one years later, Git is almost everywhere software exists. More than 90% of software projects worldwide use it. And the vast majority of people have never heard of it.

One sentence before the story: Git is a system that records every change made to a file, stores who made it, when they made it, and why, and allows you to go back to any point. It also allows multiple people to work simultaneously without stepping on each other's work.

It sounds technical that way. With a photo at the beach, it isn't.

## 9. Maria's beach photo

*Cast: João (boyfriend), Maria (girlfriend), Mariana (Maria's sister), Julia (Maria's cousin)*

João takes a photo of Maria at the beach. They went with Mariana, Maria's sister, who knows how to use filters and photo editors. This original photo is the official state of what exists at that moment — in Git vocabulary, it's called **MAIN**: the version where something only enters after review and approval. It's not an untouchable monument. It's the destination of everything that has been validated.

Maria looks at the photo and identifies four problems: people in the background, dirt on the sand, strange ocean colors, and too many clouds in the sky. These problems are issues to be solved — in Git, each would be an **ISSUE**. Maria asks Mariana to handle them. That's the **ASSIGN**: designating someone responsible.

Before starting, Mariana sets up her identity in the editor: name, export preferences, default color profile. She hasn't changed anything in the photo yet. She just registered who is working and how. In Git, this is the **CONFIG**: configuring the environment and identity before anything else.

Mariana doesn't touch the original photo. She makes a copy on her own device to work on — if she makes a mistake, the original remains intact on João's phone. This copy is the **BRANCH**: a parallel draft that doesn't compromise the official version. The distinction matters: the original stays in one place (remote), the copy in another (local).

Before beginning the adjustments, Mariana remembers she's configured her editor to run two automatic checks every time she saves any change: one verifies the file hasn't been corrupted, the other ensures the resolution is within the agreed standard. These are **HOOKS**: automatic verification actions that trigger at specific moments in the workflow, without having to remember to run them manually. They don't edit the photo. They guard the gate.

On the copy, Mariana makes the adjustments one by one and records each separately with a description of what she did and why. Removes the people. Cleans the sand. Adjusts the ocean colors. Improves the brightness. Removes the clouds. Each of these records is a **COMMIT**: a documented change, with authorship and intention. A commit isn't just "save". It's saving with a signature and an explanation.

In the middle of the process, Mariana tries a black-and-white filter. It looks interesting, but it's not for this version. She pockets it for now — in a temporary folder, outside the main work. In Git, this is the **STASH**: a quickly stored draft to avoid cluttering the current history. Important: stash is volatile. If you forget it there, it disappears.

When she finishes, Mariana sends the edited photo back to Maria to evaluate. This formal submission is the **PULL REQUEST**: the proposal that the work is ready to be reviewed and, if approved, enter the official version.

Maria looks carefully — this is the **REVIEW**. She likes everything except the ocean color adjustment. Maria asks Mariana to undo only that change, without touching the others. Mariana does exactly that: she undoes only the ocean filter by creating a new record that cancels that specific adjustment. Everything else stays. The history remains intact — it's recorded that the undoing happened, when, and why. This surgical undo that preserves history is the **REVERT**.

At this point, Julia — Maria's cousin, sitting across the table — had also made a brightness adjustment to the same area of the sky, without telling anyone. When Mariana and Julia tried to combine their work, the system stalled: which version of the sky wins? Mariana's or Julia's? Maria has to decide. This is a **MERGE CONFLICT**: two people worked on the same piece at the same time, and the system cannot choose on its own. The conflict isn't a bug. It's the system working: it refuses to merge contradictory things without an explicit human decision.

Maria approves the final result (**APPROVAL**). Before making it official, Maria tags the photo in the album as "beach june 2025" — a label that identifies this specific version for future reference. This is a **TAG**: a named milestone at a specific point on the timeline.

The approved photo becomes the new official version: the **MERGE**. The working copy is merged back into the official state, with a record of who approved it and when. Maria then sends the photo via WhatsApp to her mother — the **DEPLOY**: the publication, the sending to the world.

João and Maria save the complete folder of approved photos to their own phones. Not to edit — just to have the official version accessible at any time. In Git, this is the **CLONE**: a complete local copy of the official version.

Maria's mother receives it and finds it beautiful, but says it looks too artificial. Maria then goes back to the history and marks the previous version — no filters, no adjustments — as official. This is **ROLLBACK**: restoring a previous state entirely as the official version. It's not a specific command in Git — it's a concept. What matters is that the history remains intact. Nothing disappears. Only what's marked as official now changes. Unlike the revert, which undid a specific change by creating a new record, the rollback declares that a previous point is the current state. More drastic. Equally traceable.

Shortly after, Mariana messages Maria: "Want me to edit anything else?" This is **FETCH**: checking whether something new is available, without downloading or applying anything yet. Maria says yes, and Mariana sends the suggestions. Maria downloads and incorporates everything in one action — that's **PULL**: fetch and apply in one step. The difference between the two is small but important: fetch reads the menu, pull orders the dish and eats it.

Then Maria notices something nobody had seen: a dirt spot on her leg that made it into the already-sent photo. Mariana removes only that in the moment, with a reduced but still traceable flow — a quick branch, correction, summary approval from Maria, merge. This is **HOTFIX**: an urgent correction made after publication. The process is smaller, not nonexistent.

Finally, Julia takes the photo, creates a complete copy under her own account, and starts building an independent album — her own rules, different visual identity, a destination that may never cross Maria and João's album again — or might, someday, be incorporated back if it passes review and approval. Same historical starting point, separate governance, autonomous result. This is the **FORK**: a complete copy of the repository under new governance. It's not a parallel draft within the same project. It's another project, with a different owner, that carries the entire history of where it came from.

**The 4-question test applied**

I introduced the test earlier. Here it is in real use.

*Do the elements correspond one-to-one?* Yes. Each of the 21 Git concepts has an exact equivalent in the story, without forcing any situation and without any character left over. The table above is the evidence.

*Are the relationships between them the same?* Yes. The sequence follows the same logic in both systems: identify the problem, designate a responsible party, create a separate draft, record each change, propose formally, review, approve, publish, correct if necessary. This isn't a coincidence of order. It's the same flow architecture.

*Does the system behave the same under pressure?* Yes. Error after publication: hotfix for small surgical correction, rollback to restore the previous state entirely when the problem is general. The structural response is identical in both systems.

*Do the limits and breaking points coincide?* Yes, and this is the most revealing. Both systems depend on the discipline of those who operate them. If Maria approves without reviewing, the wrong photo goes to her mother. If the gatekeeper approves without reviewing, the wrong decision enters the official system. The failure point is the same: the process is only as reliable as the people who follow it.

The structure survives all four tests. It's not metaphor. It's functional isomorphism.

---

*One final note: Git doesn't govern anyone. It records what happens. Those who govern are the people. A versioning system makes visible what was already happening — decisions made, by whom, when, with what justification. This changes the cost of acting in bad faith. It doesn't eliminate bad faith.*

Before the series articles, there's a prior text that asks an uncomfortable question: why do democracies die from the inside, without anyone noticing, and what does that have to do with how any human group makes collective decisions? That question doesn't have an easy answer. But it has a clue that comes from software engineering, of all unlikely places.

The series is called *Governance as Versioning*. The map comes first. That's where we begin. The territory starts right after.

---

I shared parts of my story not to impress anyone. There's nothing special about being dropped into new countries without being asked. Most people go through changes just as large, or larger, in other ways.

I shared it because the thesis of this article was born there, before it had a name. In the cast that forced the left hand. In the French that needed a week to make sense. In the Peruvian Spanish that tangled with Argentine Spanish. In the Portuguese learned late, in a school that wasn't mine, in a city I didn't know.

None of those times did I start from zero. I just didn't know that yet.

What I know today is that every time I was forced to transfer an experience to a new context, I became a little less rigid. A little less afraid. A little more curious about why people do things the way they do.

It's not a merit. It's what happens when you have no other option but to understand.

Analogy inspires. Isomorphism operates.

Real knowledge transfer requires preserving four things: structure, function, dynamics, and limits. When that happens, you're not learning something new from zero. You're recognizing a shape you already know, dressed in different clothes.

Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication.

You never start from zero.
